Working title: Spectral Sabre
Genre: Mobile third-person tactical stealth shooter
Platform: Android first, iOS later if viable
Perspective: Third-person over-the-shoulder
Mode focus: Single-player tactical missions first, optional co-op later
Tone: Near-future military thriller, stealth-tech fantasy, cinematic squad operations
Core fantasy: Lead an elite four-person squad equipped with hyperspectral camouflage and
advanced battlefield systems to execute surgical operations behind enemy lines.
1. Executive Summary
Spectral Sabre is a mobile-first third-person tactical stealth shooter where the player commands a
four-person special operations unit using advanced camouflage, drone reconnaissance,
synchronized takedowns, adaptive weapons, squad-based tactics, and a lovable digital
companion robot.
The game is an original IP built around the fantasy of elite futuristic operators. It should capture
the feeling of a deliberate near-future tactical shooter without copying any existing franchise,
story, iconography, user interface, faction, weapon names, or mission structure.
The player leads Sabre Unit, a deniable black-ops squad using hyperspectral camouflage, an
experimental stealth system that bends visibility across visible light, infrared, thermal, low-light,
drone optics, and conventional camera feeds. The technology is powerful but not magic. It has
tactical rules, weaknesses, heat buildup, battery drain, environmental interference, and enemy
countermeasures.
KIP, the squad's compact digital infiltration robot, adds a warmer emotional core to the game. KIP
hacks anything digital in the field, provides intel, turns enemy technology into opportunity, and
gives the squad a strange but lovable companion to protect.
The player scouts the compound from the shadows, tags threats with a
drone, sends KIP to embarrass a security door, moves the squad into
position, activates camo, eliminates four guards at once, slips through
the perimeter, disables the alarm, extracts the target, and vanishes
before the enemy understands what happened.
2. Design Pillars
2.1 Tactical First, Shooter Second
The player should feel powerful because they planned well, not because they sprinted into a room
and outgunned everyone. Shooting should be satisfying, but the best outcomes come from
observation, positioning, timing, stealth, and squad coordination.
2.2 Stealth Tech With Rules
Hyperspectral camouflage is the signature fantasy, but it must remain readable and balanced. It
should create tense moments, not remove tension. Camo works best when the player is slow,
crouched, in shadow, or outside direct sensor range. It becomes unstable when sprinting, firing
unsuppressed weapons, taking damage, passing through water, standing near heat sources, or
entering anti-camo scan fields.

2.3 Mobile Missions, Console Mood
The game should feel cinematic and tactical, but mission lengths must respect mobile play. Core
missions should last around 3 to 8 minutes, with optional secondary objectives for deeper play.
Dense replayable tactical sandboxes are preferred over huge open worlds at launch.
2.4 Four Operators, One Mind
The player leads a squad of four. The squad should feel present, useful, and intelligent without
requiring heavy micromanagement. Commands should be quick and mobile-friendly: mark target,
suppress, breach, flank, hold, sync shot, drone scan, and extract.
2.5 KIP Gives The Game Its Heart
The squad is professional, disciplined, and lethal. KIP is brilliant, odd, emotional, and funny. KIP
prevents the tone from becoming too cold while also serving a real tactical function.
2.6 Readable Complexity
The game can include deep systems, but the UI must stay clean. Tactical information should
appear as augmented-reality overlays: threat outlines, patrol paths, sound radius, camo strength,
detection arcs, objective markers, digital hack points, and squad status.
3. High Concept
Spectral Sabre is set in a near-future world where military surveillance has advanced faster than
conventional stealth. Drones, thermal networks, biometric scanners, satellite arrays, motion grids,
and AI-controlled security systems dominate the battlefield. Traditional stealth is dead.
In response, a covert military research program creates Hyperspectral Adaptive Refraction,
nicknamed Spectral Skin. This technology allows operators to distort their signature across
multiple detection bands. They do not simply turn invisible; they become difficult to resolve.
Cameras see shimmer. Thermal sensors see ghost heat. Drones lose lock. Human eyes struggle to
track motion in low light.
The player commands Sabre Unit, a four-person deniable squad deployed where official military
action is impossible. Their missions include rescuing hostages, destroying prototype weapons,
disabling surveillance infrastructure, eliminating rogue commanders, stealing intelligence, and
stopping private military groups from weaponising spectral technology.

5. Platform Philosophy
This is not a console game squeezed onto a phone. It is a mobile tactical shooter designed around
touch input, short sessions, clear UI, and performance constraints.
Mobile Design Rules
• Missions should be completable in short sessions.
• Stealth and squad commands should reduce the need for twitch aiming.
• Touch controls must be forgiving.
• Aim assist should be intelligent but not play the game for the user.
• UI buttons should be large enough for thumbs.
• Tactical planning should be fast, visual, and satisfying.
• The player should not need ten fingers to play well.
• Auto-cover and contextual actions are preferred over complex manual inputs.
• KIP's suggestions should help the player understand tactical options without opening complex
menus.
6. Core Gameplay Loop
Mission Loop
1. Briefing - View objective, enemy type, weather, map conditions, bonus targets, and
recommended loadout.
2. Loadout - Choose operator gear, weapons, attachments, camo modules, drone abilities, squad
perks, and KIP modules.
3. Insertion - Start at a concealed entry point and receive the initial tactical objective.
4. Recon - Use drone, optics, squad sensors, and KIP to tag enemies, cameras, alarms, doors,
scanners, and patrol paths.
5. Infiltration - Move through patrols, disable sensors, avoid detection, set up squad positions, and
exploit digital systems.
6. Execution - Sync shots, stealth takedowns, hacked distractions, breaching, suppression, or cover
combat.
7. Objective Completion - Hack terminal, rescue target, plant charge, steal intel, destroy asset,
capture HVT, or extract data.

7. Player Experience Goals
The player should regularly experience moments like:
• Watching enemy patrols from a rooftop through drone vision.
• Seeing squadmates appear as faint ghost silhouettes through camo shimmer.
• Timing a four-target sync shot before a guard turns around.
• Crawling under a scanning drone while camo battery drains.
• Choosing whether to quietly disable the generator or go loud.
• Sending KIP under a gate to loop the security camera.
• Laughing when KIP says something oddly sincere during a tense hack.
• Using smoke, EMP, camo, and KIP spoofing to escape after an alarm.
• Customising a suppressed rifle for recoil, range, and stealth.
• Feeling like the squad is elite, but not invincible.

9. Camera Design
The camera should be close enough to feel cinematic but pulled back enough for tactical
awareness.
Camera States
Exploration Camera: Slightly pulled back to show the player, squad spacing, and environment.
Aim Camera: Over-the-shoulder zoom. Weapon reticle tightens. Tagged enemy outlines remain
visible.
Cover Camera: Camera shifts to cover side. Allows shoulder swap, peeking, blind fire, and
cover-to-cover movement.
Drone Camera: Elevated tactical view. Highlights enemies, alarms, cameras, digital hack points,
route options, and KIP opportunities.
KIP View: Low-to-the-ground digital infiltration camera when directly controlling KIP for special
hacks. The view should feel small, fast, and slightly comic without breaking immersion.
Cinematic Execution Camera: Very brief slow-motion for perfect sync shots or breach executions.
This should be optional or rare to avoid interrupting mobile flow.
10. Companion Robot - KIP
10.1 Working Name
KIP stands for Kinetic Intelligence Platform.
Alternative names considered: Pip, Kilo, Bishop, Glint, Tinker, Coda, Mote, and Chirp. This
document uses KIP as the current working name.
10.2 Core Concept
KIP is a compact field-support robot assigned to Sabre Unit. It is not a weapon platform first. It is a
digital infiltration companion: part hacker, part scout, part translator, part emotional support
gremlin.
KIP's gameplay role is to help the squad exploit anything digital in the environment. Cameras,
doors, drones, alarms, turrets, lights, comms arrays, biometric locks, vehicle systems, enemy
tablets, anti-camo towers, sensor grids, and security networks all become potential tools when KIP
is nearby.

KIP should feel like a beloved squad member, not just a menu with legs.
10.3 Personality Direction
KIP is inspired by the broad archetype of a strange but lovable non-human companion: brilliant,
loyal, literal-minded, enthusiastic, brave, and emotionally sincere. KIP should not copy any
existing character's exact speech patterns, catchphrases, species traits, or mannerisms. The goal
is to make Spectral Sabre's own iconic companion.
KIP should be:
• Extremely helpful
• Oddly wholesome
• Socially unusual
• Technically brilliant
• Curious about human behaviour
• Overly literal at funny moments
• Brave despite being physically small
• Emotionally attached to the squad
• Proud when hacks succeed
• Mildly offended when treated as equipment
• Excited by elegant solutions
10.4 Emotional Role
KIP adds warmth to the cold tactical world of Spectral Sabre. The soldiers are calm, professional,
and mission-focused. KIP is the little spark of weirdness that makes the squad feel alive.
KIP can celebrate clean stealth, panic politely when plans go wrong, misunderstand sarcasm,
quietly encourage the player, refer to the squad as "my humans" or "the soft team," and make
failure less frustrating through humour.
Example KIP lines:
• "Door is locked. Door is now embarrassed."
• "Camera loop active. It is watching yesterday."
• "Enemy drone sees heat. I made your heat boring."
• "Bad news: alarm is awake. Good news: I am smarter than alarm."
• "I have opened the vent. Humans love vents, yes?"
• "Stealth rating excellent. I feel proud in my circuits."
• "This plan has many bullets in it. I suggest fewer bullets."
• "Gate is open enough for heroic crouching."
• "I have defeated human curiosity."

10.7 Movement and Mannerisms
KIP's personality should be visible through animation:
• Tilts its body when confused
• Taps surfaces before hacking them
• Freezes dramatically when spotted
• Wiggles antennae when excited
• Clings to cover during gunfire
• Climbs onto terminals like a tiny mechanic
• Projects little holographic diagrams while explaining
• Hides behind Rook when explosions happen
• Rides on the player's backpack during traversal
• Does a tiny celebratory spin after a perfect hack
• Knocks twice on a locked door before breaking into it digitally
• Emits happy diagnostic chirps after a successful intrusion
10.8 Field Role
KIP can interact with cameras, alarm panels, turrets, enemy drones, door locks, security gates,
server racks, biometric scanners, power grids, vehicle systems, motion sensors, anti-camo
towers, comms relays, smart mines, patrol tablets, digital keypads, electronic safes, automated
floodlights, and enemy bodycams.

10.9 Core Abilities
Camera Loop: Makes a camera replay old footage, allowing the squad to pass through its view
cone safely.
Door Whisper: Silently opens electronic locks. Higher-security locks take longer.
Drone Hijack: Temporarily takes over an enemy drone. The player can use it to scout, distract
enemies, or crash it.
Alarm Suppression: Delays or cancels alarm activation. Useful when a guard reaches a panic
button or when a security network detects intrusion.
Turret Confusion: Makes a turret ignore Sabre Unit or target enemies briefly.
Blackout Pulse: Disables lights in a small area. This improves camo effectiveness but may
increase enemy suspicion.
Sensor Spoof: Creates false readings for thermal, motion, or anti-camo systems.
Data Bite: Extracts intel from devices. Rewards bonus currency, mission lore, enemy route data,
hidden objectives, or upgrade fragments.
Ghost Route: Highlights a low-detection route based on hacked security data.
Signal Mimic: Imitates an authorised device, ID badge, or maintenance signal to bypass digital
checkpoints.
Panic Patch: Emergency ability. If the squad is about to be detected by a digital system, KIP can
burn a cooldown to delay detection for a few seconds.
10.10 Gameplay Balance
KIP should be powerful but not unlimited.
Limits:
• Hacking takes time
• KIP can be detected by advanced systems
• Some hacks require line of sight or physical access
• Enemy technicians can reverse hacks
• Signal jammers reduce KIP's range
• Heavy combat makes KIP retreat to safety
• KIP cannot hack purely mechanical systems
• KIP has cooldowns and battery constraints
• High-risk hacks may trigger suspicion if failed
• Some devices require Echo and KIP working together
10.11 Relationship With Echo
Echo is the human systems specialist. KIP is the field robot that physically interfaces with devices.
Echo handles strategic hacking, signal analysis, and mission-wide electronic warfare. KIP handles
close-range intrusion, device manipulation, and live field improvisation.
Their dynamic could include Echo treating KIP like a colleague, KIP arguing about inefficient
human code, Echo translating KIP's rapid technical chatter, KIP occasionally solving things Echo
thought were impossible, and Echo pretending not to be emotionally attached.

10.12 Tactical Integration
KIP can be commanded through the same squad command system.
Examples:
• Tap camera -> KIP: Loop, Blind, Trace Network
• Tap door -> KIP: Unlock, Peek Sensor, Jam Alarm
• Tap turret -> KIP: Disable, Hijack, Overload
• Tap drone -> KIP: Jam, Hijack, Crash
• Tap terminal -> KIP: Extract Data, Map Network, Kill Power
• Tap scanner -> KIP: Spoof, Disrupt, Mark Safe Window
KIP can also suggest actions contextually:
• "I can open that gate quietly."
• "Camera has old firmware. Delicious."
• "Alarm panel is nervous. I can calm it."
• "Scanner field ahead. I can make fake you."
• "Turret is angry but lonely. I can redirect anger."
10.13 KIP Upgrade Tree
Infiltration Upgrades: Faster door hacks, reduced hack noise, higher-security locks, and unlocks
while moving slowly.
Drone Warfare Upgrades: Longer drone hijack duration, temporary friendly drones, drone
self-destruct, and hijacked drones tagging enemies for sync shots.
Sensor Warfare Upgrades: Better anti-camo tower disruption, improved thermal spoofing, reduced
automated detection, and decoy movement signatures.
Data Extraction Upgrades: More bonus intel, hidden objectives, loot caches, and alternate mission
routes.
Survivability Upgrades: Better stealth while moving, emergency retreat dash, one
detection-survival event, and self-repair once per mission.
Personality Modules: Cosmetic-only idle behaviours, chirps, light reactions, and small dialogue
variations. These should not be pay-to-win.
10.14 KIP Story Function
KIP may have been built from a fragment of Project Prism's autonomous infiltration research.
Unlike most military AI, KIP has developed unusual emotional modelling and attachment
behaviour.
Possible hooks:
• KIP knows more about Project Prism than it can initially access
• Meridian wants KIP because it contains a rare intrusion model
• KIP becomes afraid of being wiped or reset
• The squad slowly stops calling KIP "equipment"
• KIP's loyalty becomes important when command orders a memory purge
• KIP may be the only system able to understand enemy spectral code
• KIP discovers that some Meridian systems recognise it

11. The Squad
The player leads four operators. The squad should be distinct enough to support tactical roles
without becoming overwhelming.
11.1 Operator 1 - Wraith
Role: Adaptive commander
Speciality: Player-selected loadout
Core Function: Direct control, command issuing, mission decisions
Wraith is the player avatar and squad leader. The character can be customised visually and
mechanically over time.
11.2 Operator 2 - Vantage
Role: Long-range overwatch
Speciality: Precision shots, drone-assisted targeting, thermal optics
Vantage excels at sync shots, overwatch support, and disabling exposed targets. Vantage can be
ordered to hold a sightline, cover an area, or silently eliminate a marked guard.
11.3 Operator 3 - Rook
Role: Close-quarters assault
Speciality: Door breaching, shield module, flash charges, heavy armour
Rook handles high-risk room entries and can draw fire when stealth fails. Rook should be the most
durable squadmate but the least subtle.
11.4 Operator 4 - Echo
Role: Drone, hacking, electronic warfare
Speciality: EMP pulses, camera loops, turret hijacking, sensor spoofing
Echo gives the team its tactical advantage against modern security networks. Echo can disable
alarms, jam drones, mark enemies through walls for a limited duration, and reduce anti-camo
pressure.

13. Hyperspectral Camouflage
Hyperspectral camouflage is the defining mechanic.
13.1 Fiction
The camo system uses layered adaptive refraction panels, thermal masking gel, micro-projector
fibre, and AI-driven spectral prediction. It attempts to match the operator's visible, thermal,
infrared, and low-light profile to the environment.
It is not true invisibility. It is battlefield deception.
13.2 Gameplay Function
Camo reduces detection speed rather than making the player fully undetectable. Enemies still
notice suspicious movement, sound, shimmer, footprints, muzzle flash, bodies, doors opening,
and environmental disturbance.
13.3 Camo States
Offline: No stealth benefit. Full visibility. Used when battery is depleted or disabled.
Passive Masking: Always-on minor signature reduction. Helps at long range and in shadows.
Active Camo: Player becomes highly difficult to see. Strong battery drain. Best while crouched or
walking slowly.
Overburn: Emergency near-invisibility for a few seconds. Heavy heat buildup and cooldown
afterwards.
Disrupted: Camo flickers due to EMP, water, damage, scanner field, or overheating. Enemy
detection accelerates.
13.4 Camo Meter
The HUD should display battery charge, heat buildup, visibility risk, scanner interference, and
squad camo status.

13.5 Detection Rules
Enemies detect based on distance, line of sight, light level, player stance, movement speed,
noise, camo state, enemy alertness, enemy equipment, and environmental disruption.
13.6 Camo Weaknesses
• Sprinting causes visible distortion
• Firing creates muzzle flash and sound
• Taking damage destabilises the camo field
• Water, rain, sparks, smoke, dust, and snow can reveal outlines
• Thermal scanners can partially detect heat bleed
• Anti-camo towers create scan zones
• Elite enemies can use pulse scans
• Dead bodies and open doors still raise suspicion
13.7 Camo Upgrades
• Battery Efficiency - Longer active duration
• Thermal Suppression - Reduced detection by drones and thermal optics
• Motion Smoothing - Less shimmer while walking
• Overburn Capacity - Longer emergency invisibility
• EMP Hardening - Less disruption from electronic attacks
• Squad Sync Field - Squadmates close to player gain improved camo stability
14. Stealth System
Enemy Awareness States
Unaware: Normal patrol behaviour and no suspicion.
Suspicious: Enemy noticed movement, sound, opened door, missing guard, or sensor glitch.
Investigates location.
Searching: Enemy believes a threat exists. Patrol routes change. Flashlights, drones, and scan
pulses increase.
Alerted: Enemy has confirmed contact. Alarms may trigger. Reinforcements move in.
Combat: Full engagement. Enemies use cover, grenades, flanking, and suppression.
Lockdown: Doors lock, drones deploy, anti-camo scanners activate, and extraction becomes
harder.

Stealth Failures Should Be Recoverable
Being spotted should not always mean mission failure. The player should be able to kill the
spotter before they radio, jam the alarm, use smoke and camo to break line of sight, disable base
power, relocate and re-enter stealth, or extract early with reduced rewards.
15. Combat System
Combat should feel weighty and dangerous, but manageable on mobile.
Weapon Handling
Each weapon has damage, range, recoil, stability, noise, handling, armour penetration, magazine
size, reload speed, and suppression value.
Shooting Modes
• Hip fire
• Aim down sights
• Shoulder swap
• Blind fire from cover
• Suppressed fire
• Burst fire
• Semi-auto precision fire
Hit Feedback
Enemies should react clearly: armour hit spark, helmet deflection, suppressed body hit, stagger,
knockdown, silent takedown, or drone-disrupted stun.
Damage Philosophy
The player should not be a bullet sponge. Armour and squad support help, but poor positioning
should be punished.
Possible model:
• Armour Plate - Absorbs initial damage
• Health - Limited and dangerous to lose
• Downed State - Squadmate can revive if safe
• Permanent Mission Penalty - Downed operators reduce mission score
16. Cover System
Cover is essential to the game's identity.
Cover Types
Low Cover: Player crouches. Can vault. Vulnerable to grenades and flanks.
High Cover: Player stands against wall or barrier. Can lean and peek. Safer but restricts
movement.
Soft Cover: Bushes, smoke, curtains, darkness. Helps stealth but does not stop bullets.

17. Drone and Recon System
The drone should be one of the most important tools.
Drone Functions
• Tag enemies
• Identify patrol routes
• Detect alarms and cameras
• Scan objectives
• Mark loot/intel
• Reveal weak points
• Create sound distractions
• Emit short EMP pulse
• Hack simple devices
Drone Limitations
• Battery limited
• Can be spotted by guards
• Can be jammed
• Cannot enter shielded rooms without upgrades
• Vulnerable to anti-drone turrets
• Makes quiet electronic noise nearby

Perch Mode: Lands on wall or ceiling and acts as temporary camera.

18. Sync Shot System
The sync shot is a core tactical power moment.
Basic Function
The player marks up to four targets. Each operator lines up a shot. When all lines are ready, the
player taps execute. Targets are eliminated simultaneously if conditions are valid.
Requirements
• Target must be visible to assigned operator
• Operator must have line of sight
• Weapon must be appropriate
• Enemy armour may require headshot or armour-piercing ammo
• Moving targets increase preparation time
• Alert enemies reduce success chance
• Communication jamming may block coordination
Mobile Interaction
• Tap enemy to mark
• Long-press to assign sync shot
• HUD shows Ready indicators over each target
• Execute button appears when at least two shots are queued
Balance
Sync shot should be powerful but limited by cooldown, ammo, line of sight, squad positioning,
camo stability, enemy helmets/armour, and communication jamming.
19. Enemy Design
Enemies should push the player to use the full tactical kit.
Basic Enemy Types
Patrol Guard: Lightly armed, basic detection, vulnerable to stealth.

23. World and Story
Setting
The world is in a state of cold technological conflict. Nation-states, private military corporations,
intelligence agencies, and rogue research groups compete over the future of battlefield
invisibility.
Traditional military presence is politically impossible in many zones. Instead, governments deploy
deniable squads using experimental technologies.
Main Conflict
A private military network known as The Meridian Directorate has acquired fragments of
hyperspectral camouflage research. They are using it to create invisible kill teams, destabilise
regions, and sell stealth warfare to the highest bidder.
Sabre Unit is activated to stop Meridian before they complete a deployable mass-production
version of the technology.
Key Factions
Sabre Unit: Player squad. Elite, deniable, experimental.
The Meridian Directorate: Private military and intelligence cartel. Main antagonist. Highly
equipped and morally detached.
Civic Shield Authority: Local security forces in unstable regions. Sometimes allies, sometimes
obstacles.
Black Lantern Syndicate: Arms smugglers and data brokers. Sell tech to both sides.
Project Prism: Secret research program behind hyperspectral camouflage. Not fully trustworthy.
The Pale Choir: Mysterious rogue unit using early spectral prototypes. Possible late-game elite
enemy.

35. Monetisation Philosophy
The game should avoid pay-to-win, especially if aiming for long-term trust.
Acceptable Monetisation
• Cosmetic armour skins
• Weapon skins
• Operator outfits
• Camo visual effects
• KIP shell skins
• KIP light-core colours
• Cosmetic KIP personality modules
• Battle pass with cosmetics and resources
• Optional ad reward for extra non-premium currency
• Paid campaign expansions
• Founder pack
Risky Monetisation
Avoid selling direct combat superiority in competitive modes. If co-op or PvP arrives later, paid
power will damage the game's reputation.

For the first version, use either free-to-play with cosmetic monetisation or a low-cost premium
game with optional cosmetic DLC. Free-to-play is more realistic commercially, but it requires
careful economy design.

39. Risks and Solutions
Risk: Scope Creep
Trying to build a full console-style tactical shooter immediately will likely stall the project.
Solution: Build one polished mission first.
Risk: Mobile Controls Feel Bad
Third-person shooters can feel clumsy on touchscreens.
Solution: Use assisted aiming, auto-cover, contextual commands, and tactical mechanics that
reward planning over twitch reactions.
Risk: Camo Makes Game Too Easy
If invisibility is too strong, stealth loses tension.
Solution: Use battery, heat, shimmer, scanners, sound, environmental disruption, and enemies
designed to counter it.
Risk: Squad AI Becomes Too Complex
Full tactical AI is difficult.
Solution: Start with limited commands and strong illusion of intelligence: cover selection, sync
shots, follow, revive, suppression, and clear voice feedback.
Risk: KIP Becomes Annoying
A talkative companion can become irritating if overused.
Solution: Make KIP context-aware, helpful first, funny second, and provide chatter frequency
settings.
Risk: Performance Problems
Camo shaders, drones, AI, effects, and KIP animation can hurt mobile performance.
Solution: Profile early, use simple shader variants, keep missions compact, limit enemy counts,
and build for mid-range Android hardware.
Risk: Too Similar to Existing Franchises
A homage can accidentally become derivative.
Solution: Use original terminology, factions, UI, characters, mission themes, and technology rules.
Build the identity around hyperspectral warfare, Sabre Unit, and KIP.
